**Hack The Globe 2026: Devpost One-Pager Submission Requirements**

For Hack The Globe 2026, you are **required** to create a page on Devpost detailing your technical solution as part of your submission. This page will serve as a **supplement** to your first round presentation and will allow judges to review your technical solution and development process with a little more depth than is typically possible via the 10-minute first round presentation. This page will also be added to a repository and shared with all teams in the hackathon to provide an opportunity for hacker teams to see one another’s innovation, and vote on the People’s Choice Award amongst finalist teams. Remember that your first-round presentation **must** still cover both business and technical aspects of your solution, as this will be judged according to the rubric.

**Instructions on Submitting Devpost \+ Technical Solution**

1. Create an account on [Devpost](https://devpost.com/).  
2. After creating your account, hover over your profile icon in the top right of the homepage to see a dropdown menu. From this dropdown menu, navigate to your portfolio.  
3. From your portfolio page select the button “Add a new project”. This will lead to a prompt asking you if you are submitting to a hackathon or just to your portfolio. Select “No, I’m just adding it to my portfolio”. Give your project a name then click “Save and Continue”.  
4. You will now be on a page where you can make your Devpost submission. You can add your team members to collaborate on the post together if you’d like. Refer to the guidelines below if you are unsure what to add to your Devpost. Once your Devpost is ready click Save at the bottom of the page, you can always go back and edit your project page if you forgot anything.  
5. Once your page is complete, submit the link to your page via the Hacker Dashboard, along with your first round presentation slide deck, Github repo and any supporting technical materials. More detailed instructions on how to do this will be posted closer to the end of the solution scoping period.

**Guidelines for Writing Your Devpost**  
A typical Devpost page, which you can find many examples of on the site, includes the following sections:

1. Inspiration  
2. What does our solution do?  
3. How we built it  
4. Challenges we faced  
5. Accomplishments we’re proud of  
6. What we learned?  
7. What’s next?

**1\. Inspiration**  
Introduce the **underlying problem** and anything else which inspired you to build your solution. It might be a good idea to describe why you chose to address it in the way that you did. This can be done by comparing it to other available or considered solutions. This section need not be extremely detailed as this should likely be covered in your presentation.

**2\. What does our solution  do?**  
For this section of your Devpost you must describe how your solution works. This includes two components; **what does it do on a technical level** and **how does this lead to positive outcomes** in the broader sense of your social impact problem. You **should** include any Figma, visualizations, or technical drawings which help judges envision your technical solution. Please be as detailed as possible in this section.

It is recommended to include some visualization or interactive component of your design in this section, such as a “Try it out” link or a video demonstration.

**3\. How we built it**  
Please **describe the team’s process used for developing this solution**. The process detailed on your Devpost submission can begin **after** you have chosen your specific solution from a group of candidate designs (do not describe any considered solutions in this section).

While describing how the team built it be sure to specifically detail any softwares and/or tools used throughout the process. If your solution has multiple functions working together please detail how each function was made and incorporated together.

Additionally, if your team is looking to get full points on the *Technical Proficiency* section of the rubric you should include the results of your validation testing as well as detailing the steps you took to validate your solution. This includes describing why you chose the specific tests you did and justifying the outcomes of these tests.

**4\. Challenges we faced**  
Please detail the challenges your team faced while developing the solution. This can include both challenges you were able to overcome and those you were not able to resolve either given the timeframe or technical expertise of your team. If you include both be sure to identify which were resolved.

**5\. Accomplishments/Learnings**  
Show off the awesome aspects of your technical solution and anything you learned along the way. Reflecting on what you’re proud of is just as important as reflecting on the hurdles you faced.

**6\. What’s next?**  
There are two topics which should be covered in this section.

Firstly, describe any changes/improvements you would have made to your design if you had more time or your team had a higher baseline of technical skills. We understand this is a social impact hackathon with many competitors from non–technical backgrounds, so we’d love to hear what your design would have looked like if you had the capability. These are expected to be reasonable changes/improvements – if they would take you more than 1-2 weeks to develop working full-time to enact them, they’re probably too far-fetched\!

Secondly, describe any long-term changes which would need to be made to your design to make it the solution scalable and boost adoption. Be sure to describe how these changes are related to your current form of the design \- you should not be describing an entirely different solution here.

**Note on Length of Devpost**  
While we have decided not to impose a minimum or maximum length to the Devpost, you should remember that this document serves to compliment your business presentation. Feel free to use the space/tools Devpost provides to get creative in how you showcase your technical solution or get into the nitty gritty of your technical solution. However, please remember to be reasonable in the overall length of your Devpost, keeping in mind the audience of the page. Other hackers (who haven’t watched your presentation) may use the page as a one-stop to learn about your solution. On the other hand, judges (who have already watched your presentation) should be able to skim through and pick up the major takeaways of sections 3-6 in a couple of minutes; they will treat this as an **appendix** to your presentation and use it to better understand your **development process**.
