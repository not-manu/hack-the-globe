# EcoSync Pitch Plan

## The Story: Ontario Can't Build Fast Enough — And We're Making It Worse

Ontario needs **1.5 million new homes by 2031** (Ontario Housing Affordability Task Force, 2022). That's the most ambitious housing target in Canadian history. The province passed Bill 23 — the *More Homes Built Faster Act* — to make it happen.

But here's the thing nobody talks about: **every home we build creates waste, and that waste makes the next home more expensive.**

Construction and demolition waste makes up **25–30% of everything going into Ontario landfills** — over 2 million tonnes a year. And buried in those landfills are pallets of landscaping stone, stacks of concrete block, perfectly good brick, steel rebar, pavers — **materials that don't rot, don't degrade, and could be on someone else's job site tomorrow.**

Instead, contractors pay **$120–$170/tonne** in tipping fees to dump them. And the builder down the road pays full retail for the exact same product.

> "We're in a housing crisis. Families can't afford homes. And we're literally burying the materials that could make them cheaper."

That's the problem EcoSync solves.

## (A) The Market Is Real

### Ontario's Construction Boom = Ontario's Waste Boom

- **1.5M homes needed by 2031** — Bill 23 is accelerating permits and construction starts
- Ontario construction activity accounts for the largest share of Canada's ~4M tonnes of annual C&D waste
- The more we build, the more surplus we generate — this problem **scales with the solution to the housing crisis**
- The global C&D waste management market is valued at **$200B+** and growing at ~5% CAGR

### The Materials That Last Forever (But Get Thrown Away Anyway)

Focus on **durable, non-degrading materials** that have no business being in a landfill.

**Landscaping stone & pavers:**
- Natural stone pavers retail at **$10–$30/sq ft**. Contractors order 5–10% extra for cuts and breakage.
- Leftover pallets sit on job sites for weeks, then get dumped — because there's no easy way to sell a half-pallet of flagstone
- Stone doesn't degrade. A paver thrown in a landfill today will still be perfectly usable in 500 years
- Landscaping is part of virtually every residential build — this isn't niche, it's universal

**Concrete block & brick:**
- Concrete block: $2–$5/unit. Brick: $0.50–$1.50/unit. Cheap per piece, expensive in volume.
- A single residential project can leave behind hundreds of unused blocks
- Concrete is one of the most recyclable C&D materials (~70–80% reuse rate when separated) — but separation rarely happens on small job sites
- Reclaimed brick has a growing market (heritage look, sustainability appeal) but no efficient source

**Steel & rebar:**
- Steel is the most recycled material on earth (~90% global recycling rate) — proving demand exists
- But small contractors get lowballed at scrapyards. No price transparency, no competition
- Structural steel and rebar are standardized, high-value, and easy to verify

### The Gap

Right now, a contractor with leftover stone has three options: dump it and pay tipping fees, post it on Kijiji and hope someone wants exactly what they have, or let it sit on the job site taking up space. Meanwhile, a homeowner doing a backyard patio needs 40 pavers — but there's no way to find that contractor.

**There's no system that connects the people throwing materials away with the people who need them.**

## (B) This Is a Big Problem — And It Hits Home

### It Makes Housing More Expensive

- Materials account for **40–50% of total home construction costs** (CHBA)
- Ontario construction material costs rose **30–50% between 2020 and 2024** (Statistics Canada Building Construction Price Index)
- The average Ontario home now costs ~**$850,000+** (CREA). Every dollar saved on materials matters.
- Surplus materials being dumped instead of resold means builders eat the cost — and pass it to buyers

### It Fills Our Landfills With Things That Never Break Down

- C&D waste takes up **~25–30% of Ontario landfill space**
- Stone, concrete, brick, and steel **do not decompose** — they sit in landfills permanently
- Ontario Regulation 103/94 requires source separation of C&D waste on larger sites — but compliance is inconsistent and enforcement is weak
- Toronto's Chapter 844 bylaw requires **70% C&D diversion from landfill** for demolition projects — but there aren't enough channels to actually divert to

### It Hurts the People Who Can Least Afford It

This is where the story gets personal:

> A young couple in Brampton saves for years to afford their first home. The builder quotes them $40,000 for landscaping. Meanwhile, three streets over, a contractor just finished a project and is paying $150/tonne to dump perfectly good stone pavers into a landfill. Nobody connected them. That $40,000 could have been $25,000.

> A homeowner in Mississauga wants to redo their backyard patio. They need 50 square feet of stone pavers. Home Depot sells them at $15/sq ft — that's $750. But a landscaper 10 minutes away just finished a job and has 200 sq ft of the same stone sitting on a pallet with nowhere to go. That homeowner could have paid $400 instead of $750 — if only someone connected them.

> A small GC in Hamilton is trying to bid competitively on affordable housing projects. He can't — because his material costs are the same as the big developers, but he doesn't have their margins. If he could source surplus stone and block at 30–50% off retail, he'd win more bids and build more homes.

This isn't just an environmental problem. **It's an affordability problem disguised as a waste problem.**

### The Triple Crisis (for the judges)

| Crisis | What's happening | Who it hurts |
|---|---|---|
| **Housing** | Ontario needs 1.5M homes but costs keep rising | Families priced out of homeownership |
| **Waste** | Construction surplus goes to landfill because there's no resale channel | Ontario landfills filling with permanent materials |
| **Cost** | Builders pay to dump materials; other builders pay full price for the same thing | Small builders, homeowners, and DIYers squeezed |

EcoSync sits at the intersection of all three.

## (C) Case Studies & Proof Points

### 1. Habitat for Humanity ReStores (Ontario has 90+ locations)
- Accept donated building materials and sell them to the public at discount
- Massive demand — ReStores generate **$100M+ in annual revenue across Canada**
- **Proves:** people WANT to buy surplus construction materials at a discount
- **Why we're different:** ReStores rely on donations (unpredictable supply), have no online inventory (you have to drive there and hope), and take everything (unfocused). EcoSync is digital-first — you see what's available before you leave the house.

### 2. Rheaply (Chicago) — B2B Reuse Marketplace
- Raised **$18.75M+** in venture funding
- Helps organizations redistribute surplus assets (office furniture, lab equipment, etc.)
- **Proves:** the surplus reuse model is venture-backable and scalable
- **Our angle:** Rheaply is broad and enterprise-focused. EcoSync is community-focused — contractors, homeowners, and DIYers trading durable construction materials locally.

### 3. Facebook Marketplace / Kijiji (The Accidental Proof)
- People already post surplus construction materials on Facebook Marketplace and Kijiji every day
- These listings get responses — **the demand is there**
- But the experience is terrible: no material verification, no search by material type, no carbon tracking, no trust system, listings buried under furniture and baby clothes
- EcoSync is what happens when you build a purpose-built platform for this specific use case

### 4. EC3 — Embodied Carbon in Construction Calculator
- Free, open-source database backed by Building Transparency Foundation
- Contains EPD data for **100K+ construction products**
- **We integrate with this** — every EcoSync transaction auto-calculates carbon saved
- **Why this matters:** makes our carbon tracking real engineering, not hand-waving

### 5. Ontario's Own Regulatory Push
- **O. Reg 103/94:** mandates C&D source separation on larger sites
- **Toronto Chapter 844:** requires 70% C&D diversion from landfill
- **Bill 23:** accelerating construction = accelerating waste generation
- **Proves:** contractors need somewhere to divert materials to. We give them somewhere.

### 6. Past HTG Winners Validate the Pattern
- **2025 2nd Place:** second-hand clothing marketplace (surplus goods resale)
- **2024 1st Place:** EcoTrack — smart composters (sustainability + data tracking)
- **2023 1st Place:** BagSHARE — reusable bag sharing (circular economy)
- Focused, circular economy solutions win at Hack the Globe

## (D) How EcoSync Works — The Hybrid Model

### The Big Idea

EcoSync starts as a **community marketplace with a request board** — lightweight, no warehouses, no trucks. People with surplus sell directly to people who need it. But every listing and every request is data. When we see enough demand in one area, we open a depot and start buying and holding materials ourselves.

> "Day one, we're a matchmaker — connecting surplus to demand. But every request and every listing is data. When we see 50 requests for stone pavers in Brampton in one month, that's when we open a depot. The marketplace tells us exactly where to be and what to stock."

**Two sides of the app:**

**Seller side — "Don't dump it. List it."**
- Snap a photo of surplus materials
- AI identifies material type, estimates quantity and condition, suggests a fair price
- Listing goes live with GPS location
- Get notified when a nearby buyer wants what you have
- Sell and get paid through the app — beats paying tipping fees every time

**Buyer side — "Post what you need."**
- Post a request: what material, how much, what area
- Browse available surplus on a map — filter by material, quantity, distance, price
- Get notified when someone near you lists what you're looking for
- Buy at 30–60% below retail directly from the seller
- Every purchase comes with a carbon savings receipt

**The Request Board is the secret weapon:**
- Buyers post what they need BEFORE it's available — "I need 100 bricks in Scarborough"
- When a contractor nearby lists matching surplus, both sides get notified instantly
- This solves the timing problem — the request waits for the supply, not the other way around
- And it generates **demand data** we can see in real-time: what materials, what quantities, what neighbourhoods

**Carbon Ledger — built into every transaction:**
- Every sale auto-calculates carbon emissions avoided (via EC3 data)
- Sellers get a diversion report (helps with Toronto's 70% bylaw compliance)
- Buyers get a carbon savings receipt
- Contractors can export compliance-ready reports for permits and green building incentives

### Phase 2: The Depot Model (Where We Scale)

The marketplace generates data. The data tells us where to open depots.

> "If we see 200 requests for stone and brick in the GTA in a month, we know there's enough demand to justify a pickup truck and a small warehouse. Now we can buy surplus proactively, hold inventory, and sell in any quantity."

**How depots work:**
- We buy surplus from contractors at ~20–30% of retail (still cheaper than their tipping fees)
- Sort, quality-check, and catalog at the depot
- Resell at ~50–70% of retail in any quantity — 10 pavers or 1,000
- **30–40% gross margins** because our cost basis is near zero

**Why this is a natural evolution, not a pivot:**
- Phase 1 proves demand and maps it geographically
- Phase 2 uses that demand data to place depots where they'll succeed
- The request board becomes the depot's purchasing signal — we stock what people are already asking for
- No guesswork. No "build it and hope they come."

### Why This Hybrid Beats Either Model Alone

| Challenge | Pure Marketplace | Pure Depot | EcoSync Hybrid |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Startup cost** | Low | High (warehouse + truck) | **Starts low, invests only where data supports it** |
| **Cold start** | Hard — need both sides | Easier — just need sellers | **Request board captures demand before supply exists** |
| **Quantity mismatch** | Buyer needs 20, seller has 200 | Depot breaks bulk | **Marketplace for big matches, depot for flexible quantities** |
| **Quality trust** | Stranger's photos | Depot inspects | **Marketplace has AI verification + ratings; depot adds human inspection** |
| **Data** | Limited to completed transactions | Limited to what you stock | **Request board shows unmet demand — the most valuable signal** |
| **Scalability** | Scales easily but thin margins | Scales but capital-heavy | **Marketplace scales everywhere; depots only where proven** |

### Business Model

**Phase 1 (Marketplace):**

| Revenue Stream | How It Works | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| **Transaction fee** | 10% commission on every sale | ~$30–150 per transaction |
| **Carbon reporting (SaaS)** | Contractors subscribe for compliance exports | $49–199/month per project |
| **Featured listings** | Sellers pay to boost visibility | $5–15 per listing |

**Phase 2 (Depot — when data supports it):**

| Revenue Stream | How It Works | Margin |
|---|---|---|
| **Buy low, sell higher** | Buy at ~20–30% of retail, sell at ~50–70% | **30–40% gross margin** |
| **Pickup/delivery** | Charge for job-site collection or buyer delivery | Covers logistics + margin |
| **Carbon reporting (SaaS)** | Same as Phase 1, now with depot-verified data | $49–199/month |

**Example unit economics (landscaping stone, Phase 2):**
- Retail price: $15/sq ft
- We buy from contractor: $3/sq ft
- We resell to buyer: $8/sq ft (47% savings for them)
- **Our margin: $5/sq ft**
- A single pallet (~100 sq ft) = **$500 gross profit**

### Why It's Credible

- **Phase 1 costs almost nothing to run** — it's a web app. No warehouse, no truck, no inventory risk.
- **The request board is a built-in demand radar** — we know what to stock before we stock it.
- **Phase 2 follows the scrapyard model** — proven for a century. Buy cheap, sort, sell at markup.
- **Materials don't degrade** — stone, brick, concrete, steel. No shelf life. No spoilage. No inventory loss.
- **Scales with the housing boom** — more construction = more surplus = more supply. The crisis IS our supply chain.
- **Integrates real data** — EC3 carbon tracking, not made-up numbers.
- **Regulatory tailwind** — Ontario's diversion mandates mean contractors need somewhere to send materials. We're that somewhere.
